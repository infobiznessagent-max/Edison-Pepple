CAMPUS REPORT

Structured market intelligence gathering and synthesis to uncover viable operational opportunities and mitigate structural risks across complex educational ecosystems.

Nigeria’s government tertiary institutions serve millions of students navigating fragmented and underserved daily needs. Despite high smartphone usage and strong peer communities, no integrated digital platform currently serves student needs holistically.

Campus Report was designed to identify high-signal product opportunities within this ecosystem and translate field research into actionable product directions.

01

The Challenge

The Problem

University students navigate critical needs daily:
• sourcing course materials
• finding accommodation
• buying and selling used goods
• accessing academic support
• managing financial behavior

Yet most workflows remain offline, fragmented, informal, and trust-deficient. The Fidel Team needed to understand:
Where are the strongest product opportunities inside this ecosystem?

Key Business Questions
  • What student problems are structurally underserved?
  • Which problems show strongest willingness-to-pay?
  • Where does digital adoption already exist?
  • Which opportunity spaces are most viable for product development?
02

Research Approach

This was an exploratory qualitative research study conducted across 5 government tertiary institutions in Nigeria. Patterns began stabilizing by participant 10.

Research Methods
  • Semi-structured interviews
  • Behavioral observation
  • Pattern clustering
  • Opportunity synthesis
Participant Focus

Students across:
• multiple levels
• faculties
• campuses
• housing conditions
• academic contexts

03

Key Insights

01

Digital learning is not primarily a technology problem.
Students strongly prefer physical and socially interactive learning environments. The barrier to digital learning adoption is largely social, not infrastructural.

Implication: Future edtech products must design for peer interaction and community-driven learning.

02

Academic content discovery is broken.
Students struggle to find course-relevant materials online. General search engines create noise, friction, and mismatched resources.

Implication: There is opportunity for curated, institution-aware academic discovery.

03

Campus commerce is active but trust-deficient.
Students frequently buy and sell goods internally. Scams are common, especially for electronics and used items.

Implication: Trust and verification layers are critical to marketplace viability.

04

Accommodation discovery is structurally inefficient.
Students rely on physical scouting, fragmented referrals, and offline networks. Roommate compatibility and trust are major hidden constraints.

Implication: Housing opportunity extends beyond listings into compatibility and area intelligence.

05

WhatsApp is campus infrastructure.
Android dominates student device behavior. WhatsApp functions as the operational layer for communication and coordination.

Implication: Any product entering this ecosystem should align with mobile-first, WhatsApp-native behavior.

04

Opportunity Landscape

The research surfaced multiple product opportunity zones.

Opportunity 01

Course-indexed academic library

Opportunity 02

Peer tutoring marketplace

Opportunity 03

Student accommodation + roommate discovery platform

Opportunity 04

Campus peer commerce marketplace

Opportunity 05

Embedded student savings ecosystem

05

Strategic Thesis

The Nigerian campus ecosystem is not underserved because of lack of demand.

It is underserved because existing student workflows remain:
• fragmented
• informal
• trust-dependent
• operationally inefficient

This creates multiple adjacent opportunities for vertically integrated student products.

09

Key Learnings

Large markets often hide opportunities inside inefficient everyday workflows.

The strongest opportunities are rarely where markets appear obvious. They emerge where behavior is repeated, underserved, and trust-dependent.
